# SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Bottom Line Up Front.** The City of Chicago requires a durable, multi-year vehicle for the preventive maintenance, testing, repair, and 24/7 emergency response of its fleet of emergency and standby diesel and natural-gas generators located across municipal facilities — fire houses, police stations, 911/311 centers, water and wastewater pumping stations, traffic management facilities, shelters, and critical administrative buildings. Emergency generators are life-safety assets regulated by the National Fire Protection Association (NFPA) Standard 110 (Emergency and Standby Power Systems), NFPA 37 (Stationary Combustion Engines and Gas Turbines), NFPA 99 (where healthcare-adjacent), and NFPA 70 / the Chicago Electrical Code (Title 14E), and subject to federal and state air-quality regulation under the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) New Source Performance Standards (NSPS) for Reciprocating Internal Combustion Engines (RICE), the National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants (NESHAP / RICE MACT), and Illinois Environmental Protection Agency (IEPA) air permitting under 35 Illinois Administrative Code Part 217.

The market for qualified generator maintenance providers in the Chicago metropolitan area is a well-developed, moderately concentrated, multi-tier market. It is dominated by a small number of Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) authorized dealers — Altorfer Cat (Caterpillar, Northern and Central Illinois), Cummins Sales and Service (Illinois/Indiana region), Patten Industries (Cat, now consolidated under Altorfer in Northern Illinois), MPower (authorized Kohler and Generac), and Trusted Power Systems (multi-brand authorized service for Cummins, Kohler, Generac, Briggs & Stratton) — supplemented by independent service firms (Affiliated Customer Service Inc., Penco Electrical Contractor, Four Seasons Electric, NorthShore Power Electric) and national cooperative-contract holders reachable through Sourcewell, OMNIA Partners, and the Suburban Purchasing Cooperative.

The recommended procurement method is a **Request for Proposals (RFP)** to establish a multi-year, multi-vendor Master Consultant / Term Services Agreement structured in brand-based or geographic lots, awarded on a best-value basis. This approach is explicitly authorized under Chicago Municipal Code (MCC) Chapter 2-92 and supports the 26% Minority Business Enterprise (MBE) and 6% Women Business Enterprise (WBE) participation goals established under MCC § 2-92-420 et seq. Where immediate continuity-of-service risk exists on a specific OEM fleet before a new RFP is awarded, a cooperative piggyback under Sourcewell Contract No. 092222-CAT (Caterpillar / Carolina Cat — Electrical Energy Power Generation Equipment) or Sourcewell Contract No. 120617-CMM (Cummins) is a defensible bridge that satisfies MCC § 2-92-600 (Joint Procurement).

## Key Findings

- **Scope Validation:** The service scope spans seven distinct functional areas — preventive maintenance (PM) visits, annual load bank testing, fuel system service including fuel polishing and ASTM D975 laboratory fuel testing, emissions testing and RICE NESHAP recordkeeping, 24/7 emergency repair response, parts and warranty support, and automatic transfer switch (ATS) maintenance — and must cover mixed OEM fleets (Caterpillar, Cummins, Kohler, Generac, Detroit Diesel, MTU, Onan).
- **Regulatory Framework:** NFPA 110 Chapter 8 governs the maintenance program (weekly inspection, monthly ≥30-minute load-level exercise at ≥30% of nameplate, annual 4-hour load bank test every 36 months for Level 1 systems, annual ATS major maintenance plus three quarterly inspections); NFPA 37 governs fire-safety installation conditions; EPA RICE NSPS (40 CFR Part 60 Subparts IIII/JJJJ) and RICE NESHAP (40 CFR Part 63 Subpart ZZZZ) govern emissions and recordkeeping; and IEPA requires air construction and operating permits under 35 Ill. Adm. Code Part 217 with a Registration of Smaller Sources (ROSS) pathway for engines under 1,118 kW. The Chicago Electrical Code (MCC Title 14E, Section 14E-7-700 — Emergency Systems) requires listing under UL 2200 and sets inspection requirements, and the Chicago Building Code requires an annual full-load test witnessed by a City of Chicago Electrical Bureau Inspector.
- **Market Structure:** The market is OEM-authorized-dealer-led with meaningful independent and specialty competition. Caterpillar coverage in Chicago is held by Altorfer Cat (which now encompasses the former Patten Industries footprint in Northern Illinois); Cummins coverage is provided directly by Cummins Sales and Service; Kohler and Generac coverage is provided by MPower, Trusted Power Systems, and regional independents. Fuel polishing and ASTM fuel testing are a secondary but distinct market tier (Earthsafe, Precision Filtration, Dieselcraft, CommTank, Iowa Central Fuel Testing Laboratory).
- **Peer Benchmark:** New York City Department of Citywide Administrative Services (DCAS) maintains a multi-year citywide requirements contract for complete service, inspection, and testing of generator sets and related equipment across all five boroughs, with both scheduled and emergency work. Chicago Housing Authority (CHA) contracts separately for approximately 80 elevator-backup generators under a dedicated RFP with an annual witnessed full-load test requirement. Los Angeles County and the State of Illinois follow similar annual/semi-annual/quarterly tiered inspection models aligned to NFPA 110 and NFPA 111.
- **Vendor Pool Identified:** A qualified vendor pool of 12 firms has been identified spanning OEM-authorized dealers, multi-brand authorized service firms, independent specialty providers, and fuel-system specialists (detailed in Section 4). All must be verified for current certifications and diversity status before solicitation.
- **Diversity Opportunity:** The M/WBE opportunity is strongest in the sub-contracted tiers of the scope — fuel polishing and ASTM fuel testing, ATS electrical inspection sub-work, and parts delivery — rather than in OEM-authorized service, which is structurally concentrated. The solicitation should establish MBE/WBE participation goals at or above the city's 26%/6% contract-specific levels with an aggressive but realistic subcontracting plan. The Disadvantaged Business Enterprise (DBE) and Business Enterprise for Persons with Disabilities (BEPD) pools should also be searched in the Illinois Unified Certification Program (UCP) Directory and the City of Chicago M/WBE Directory.

## Ideal Procurement Method

**Request for Proposals (RFP)** — multi-year Term Services Agreement with geographic or OEM-brand lots, best-value award.

The recommended method is an RFP because the scope mixes commodity-like preventive maintenance work (where pricing transparency matters) with judgment-intensive tasks where vendor capability, response time, technician certification, parts inventory depth, and OEM authorization directly determine service quality. An RFP allows Chicago to evaluate price alongside technical response plans, OEM authorization evidence, response-time commitments, M/WBE subcontracting plans, and safety record — which is the definition of best value under MCC § 2-92. An Invitation for Bids (IFB) would force the city to treat a life-safety scope as a pure low-bid commodity, which is inappropriate for this risk profile and would shift execution risk to departments. A cooperative piggyback (Sourcewell 092222-CAT or Sourcewell 120617-CMM) under MCC § 2-92-600 remains a legally defensible complementary vehicle for single-OEM fleets where a competitive RFP cannot be issued within the required timeline.

# SECTION 2: PHASE 2 — INTERNAL DISCOVERY & CURRENT STATE ANALYSIS

## Emergency Generator Service Specifications and Scope

Municipal emergency generator maintenance is not a single service; it is a bundled program of seven service categories that must be specified with precision in any solicitation. The table below defines the scope categories, the regulatory driver, and the typical frequency for a Level 1 NFPA 110 system.

| **Service Category** | **Regulatory Driver** | **Typical Frequency (NFPA 110, Level 1)** |
|---|---|---|
| Preventive Maintenance (PM) visits — fluids, filters, belts, hoses, battery, charger, block heater, control panel, alarm verification | NFPA 110 Ch. 8; OEM manuals | Semi-annual (2/year) at minimum; quarterly for Level 1 critical |
| Weekly visual inspection / monthly exercise run | NFPA 110 § 8.4.1 | Weekly visual; monthly under load ≥30% nameplate for ≥30 minutes |
| Annual / triennial load bank testing | NFPA 110 § 8.4.2 | Annual if monthly exercise fails 30% load; triennial 4-hour full-load test (every 36 months) for all Level 1 |
| Fuel system service: polishing, water removal, ASTM D975 fuel sampling and laboratory analysis | NFPA 110 § 8.3.7; ASTM D975 | Annual fuel sample and lab analysis; polish on failed test or every 3–5 years |
| Emissions testing and RICE NESHAP recordkeeping | 40 CFR 63 Subpart ZZZZ; IEPA 35 IAC 217 | Annual recordkeeping; permit-dependent stack test schedule |
| 24/7 emergency repair response | Operational / contractual | On-call; 2–4 hour response Service Level Agreement (SLA) typical |
| Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS) maintenance | NFPA 110 § 8.4.6 | One major maintenance plus three quarterly inspections annually |

# SECTION 5: PHASE 4 — STRATEGIC ANALYSIS & PROCUREMENT METHOD RECOMMENDATION

## Strategic Analysis

Emergency generator maintenance is a scope where three factors determine the right procurement method: (1) the service is life-safety, and sole-price-based selection is a poor fit for life-safety services; (2) the underlying equipment fleet is multi-brand, which rules out any single-OEM-authorized solution unless the City accepts fleet fragmentation or deliberate vendor consolidation risk; and (3) response-time SLAs, parts depth, and NFPA 110-compliant testing credentials vary meaningfully between vendors and directly affect the City's exposure to a failed emergency run at a 911/311 center, a firehouse, a pumping station, or a shelter. The right answer is therefore a best-value competitive selection.

Bundling vs. unbundling deserves special attention. A single citywide award maximizes administrative efficiency and contract-management simplicity but concentrates risk and limits MBE/WBE participation. A multi-lot award structured by OEM brand (Caterpillar lot, Cummins lot, Kohler/Generac/other lot) or by geography (North/South/Central) preserves competition, enables MBE/WBE prime bidding on at least one lot, and avoids forcing a Cat-only dealer to subcontract Cummins work or vice versa. The recommended posture is **unbundling into 2–3 lots** with a preference structure that allows one prime to bid all lots but prevents any single prime from winning all lots without a compelling best-value case.

**RECOMMENDATION:** Issue a Request for Proposals (RFP) for Emergency Generator Preventive Maintenance, Testing, and Emergency Repair Services, structured as a 3-year base term with two 1-year option renewals, awarded in lots on a best-value basis. Where a continuity-of-service bridge is needed before the RFP award, use a cooperative piggyback under Sourcewell 092222-CAT (Caterpillar fleet) or Sourcewell 120617-CMM (Cummins fleet) pursuant to MCC § 2-92-600.

## Rationale

- **Life-Safety Risk Profile:** Emergency generators support 911 communications, fire stations, pumping stations, and shelters. A low-bid award that selects an under-qualified provider creates unacceptable exposure.
- **Multi-Brand Fleet Reality:** The City's fleet includes Caterpillar, Cummins, Kohler, Generac, and other OEMs. A single-award IFB would force cross-brand service that sacrifices OEM warranty and parts responsiveness.
- **NFPA and EPA Compliance Is Non-Negotiable:** Vendor selection must consider NFPA 110 Chapter 8 maintenance credentials, RICE NESHAP recordkeeping competence, and IEPA permit-compliance experience — none of which is measurable by price alone.
- **M/WBE Alignment:** An RFP allows the City to weight the M/WBE subcontracting plan as a scored evaluation factor; an IFB cannot. Lot-based award structure materially increases prime M/WBE participation opportunity.
- **Cooperative Fallback Is Legally Defensible:** MCC § 2-92-600 authorizes joint procurement. Sourcewell competitively solicits and publicly awards contracts using applicable procurement laws. This provides a compliant bridge if the RFP timeline slips.
- **Best-Value Scoring Aligns With Chicago's Existing Practice:** RFPs are an authorized procurement type under MCC Chapter 2-92 and are the city's standard vehicle for services where capability materially differs among bidders.

## Regulatory Code Compliance

### Relevant Code Sections

Under the **Chicago Municipal Code**, the following provisions apply to this solicitation:

- **MCC § 2-92-040** — Contracts for supplies, materials, equipment and services; authorizes the Chief Procurement Officer to let contracts and governs the general procurement authority.
- **MCC § 2-92-100 through § 2-92-200 series** — Procurement Fundamentals; defines procurement methods including competitive bidding (IFB), Requests for Proposals (RFP), Requests for Qualifications (RFQ), Small Orders, Job Order Contracts (JOC), and Non-Competitive procurement.
- **MCC § 2-92-410 through § 2-92-440 (MBE/WBE Program)** — Establishes the citywide 26% MBE and 6% WBE participation aspirational goal and the contract-specific goal-setting process that will govern the solicitation.
- **MCC § 2-92-412** — Bid preference for city-based businesses.
- **MCC § 2-92-600** — Joint procurement; authorizes the use of cooperative contracts with other governmental units, which is the legal basis for piggybacking Sourcewell awards.
- **MCC § 2-92-617** — Competitive bidding contracts with multiple vendors; authorizes multi-vendor award structures that support the lot-based strategy recommended above.
- **Non-Competitive Review Board (NCRB) process** under the Chief Procurement Officer's published rules — governs any sole-source or emergency award. NCRB applications must be posted for three weeks before board consideration.
- **MCC Title 14E, Section 14E-7-700 (Emergency Systems)** and the Chicago Building Code — require UL 2200 listing for stationary emergency and standby generators; set the annual witnessed load test requirement (historically cited as § 18-27-701.35 before the 2018 migration of the electrical code to Title 14E).
- **Illinois Municipal Purchasing Act** — the state statutory framework within which MCC Chapter 2-92 operates.

### Alignment Analysis

An RFP issued under MCC § 2-92 with MBE/WBE goals set under § 2-92-420 et seq. and awarded on a best-value basis is fully aligned with the Chicago Municipal Code. The recommended multi-vendor lot structure is explicitly authorized by § 2-92-617. The recommended Sourcewell bridge vehicle is authorized by § 2-92-600. The required annual witnessed full-load test (MCC Title 14E and the Chicago Building Code) must be written into the scope of work and reflected in the vendor's pricing schedule. All vendors proposed for award must clear NCRB review if the path is non-competitive for any lot.

### Conflicts and Justification

No material conflicts exist between the recommended RFP method and the Chicago Municipal Code. The only circumstance that would require a documented deviation is an emergency award under MCC § 2-92-040 for a single failed generator exceeding $500,000 — a circumstance outside the scope of this routine-maintenance solicitation. If the City chooses to use a sole-source award for a single-OEM-fleet segment on the grounds that only one authorized dealer exists in the service territory (e.g., Cat fleet in Northern Illinois served only by Altorfer), that deviation must be justified to the NCRB with a documented market-search record; a cooperative piggyback under § 2-92-600 is generally a cleaner path than a sole-source determination.

# SECTION 6: NEXT STEPS & RECOMMENDATIONS

## Immediate Actions

1. Direct user departments to validate the citywide inventory of emergency generators (make, model, kW, fuel, NFPA level, install year, last load bank date, current service vendor) and reconcile to the asset management system within 30 days.
2. Issue a Request for Information (RFI) to the qualified vendor pool to confirm current capacity, response-time commitments, and interest in lot-based participation. The RFI doubles as price-discovery for the Independent Government Cost Estimate (IGCE).
3. Initiate the MBE/WBE contract-specific goal-setting review with the DPS Office of Business Diversity and Economic Inclusion.
4. Pre-screen all Tier 1 and Tier 2 vendors against the City M/WBE Directory, Illinois UCP Directory, and Cook County Certified Vendors database; capture debarment/suspension status from SAM.gov and the Illinois Procurement Bulletin.
5. If immediate bridging service is required for a specific OEM fleet, prepare a MCC § 2-92-600 cooperative piggyback using Sourcewell 092222-CAT or 120617-CMM and submit through the standard joint procurement approval process.

## Solicitation Development Recommendations

1. **Scope of Work** — organize by the seven service categories in Section 2; specify NFPA 110 Chapter 8 frequencies as floors, not ceilings; require annual witnessed full-load test per MCC Title 14E; require ASTM D975 laboratory fuel sampling and results reporting per NFPA 110 § 8.3.7; require RICE NESHAP recordkeeping templates.
2. **Pricing Structure** — mandate a unit-pricing schedule covering: quarterly and semi-annual PM visits by kW tier; T&M hourly labor rates for regular, after-hours (defined as outside 7:00 a.m.–5:00 p.m. weekdays), weekend, and holiday; mobilization/truck-roll fee; load bank test pricing by kW tier (up to 500 kW, 501–1,000 kW, 1,001–2,000 kW); fuel polishing per gallon or per tank; ATS major maintenance flat rate; parts at a disclosed markup over published OEM list.
3. **Minimum Qualifications** — current OEM authorization for each bid lot; Electrical Generating Systems Association (EGSA) technician certifications in the response team; minimum five (5) years in business; active Illinois electrical contractor credentials; OSHA 10 or 30 hour training; current general liability, auto, and workers' compensation insurance at City limits; EMR at or below 1.0; no active debarment or suspension.
4. **Diversity Goals** — set contract-specific MBE participation at 26% and WBE at 6%, consistent with MCC § 2-92-420 series; require a written subcontracting plan and a Schedule C-1 for each credited subcontractor; allow MBE/WBE prime credit for lot wins by certified firms.
5. **Contract Term** — base term of three (3) years with two (2) one-year option renewals; annual escalator tied to the Bureau of Labor Statistics Producer Price Index for "Repair and Maintenance Services" capped at 3% per year unless justified by OEM parts inflation.
6. **Performance Metrics (SLAs)** — emergency response ≤2 hours for critical sites (911, water pumping, fire stations) and ≤4 hours for standard sites; on-time PM visit completion ≥98%; first-call resolution ≥85%; monthly reporting with a citywide dashboard; liquidated damages schedule for missed response windows.

- **Semi-annual PM visit, small commercial/industrial generator (under 150 kW):** $350–$750 per visit
- **Quarterly PM visit, 150–500 kW generator:** $550–$1,200 per visit
- **Quarterly PM visit, 500 kW–1 MW generator:** $900–$2,000 per visit
- **T&M labor, regular hours, OEM-authorized technician:** $150–$225 per hour

- **Mobilization / truck roll:** $125–$350 per visit outside scheduled PM

# APPENDIX A — REGULATORY REFERENCE

This appendix collects the primary regulatory instruments governing the scope. It is not an exhaustive compliance manual; it is a pointer for solicitation drafters and contract administrators.

**Fire and Life-Safety Standards**
- NFPA 110, *Standard for Emergency and Standby Power Systems* — Chapter 8 (Routine Maintenance and Operational Testing) — the single most important maintenance-program reference; prescribes weekly inspection, monthly load exercise ≥30% nameplate for ≥30 minutes, annual 4-hour load bank test every 36 months for Level 1 systems, annual ATS major maintenance plus three quarterly inspections, annual circuit breaker exercise, and fuel sampling/testing.
- NFPA 37, *Standard for the Installation and Use of Stationary Combustion Engines and Gas Turbines* — fire-safety installation and operation, including separation from structures and enclosure requirements.
- NFPA 99, *Health Care Facilities Code* — applies to any healthcare-adjacent city asset; interacts with NFPA 110 for Essential Electrical Systems.
- NFPA 70, *National Electrical Code* — adopted and amended by the Chicago Electrical Code (MCC Title 14E).

**Federal Environmental Regulation**
- 40 CFR Part 60 Subpart IIII (NSPS for Stationary Compression Ignition Internal Combustion Engines) and Subpart JJJJ (NSPS for Stationary Spark Ignition Internal Combustion Engines).
- 40 CFR Part 63 Subpart ZZZZ (NESHAP / RICE MACT) for Reciprocating Internal Combustion Engines.
- EPA Tier 4 Final emissions standards (fully implemented 2015) for non-road and certain stationary engines.

**State Environmental Regulation**
- 35 Illinois Administrative Code Part 217 (Nitrogen Oxides Emissions) — Illinois EPA-administered air rules applicable to stationary RICE units.
- Illinois EPA Registration of Smaller Sources (ROSS) program — available pathway for engines under 1,118 kW (1,500 bhp) not otherwise subject to 35 IAC 217.388(a) or (b).
- Illinois EPA Lifetime Operating Permit program — relevant for long-term emergency generator installations.

**Occupational Safety**
- OSHA 29 CFR Part 1910 — General Industry standards including Electrical (Subpart S), Lockout/Tagout (1910.147), and PPE (Subpart I).
- OSHA 29 CFR Part 1926 — Construction standards, applicable where service work involves any construction-category activity.

**Local Regulation**
- Chicago Municipal Code Title 14E, Section 14E-7-700 (Emergency Systems) — current electrical code provisions for emergency and standby power.
- Chicago Municipal Code Title 14B, Chapter 27 — Building Code electrical provisions cross-referenced to Title 14E.
- Chicago Municipal Code Chapter 2-92 — Department of Procurement Services.
- Cook County Department of Environmental Control air rules — where applicable in Cook County facilities outside the City of Chicago proper.
